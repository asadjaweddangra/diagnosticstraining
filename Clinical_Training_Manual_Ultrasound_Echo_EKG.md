# 1. Orientation & Fundamentals

## 1.1 What Is Ultrasound, Echo, and EKG? (Plain English First)

### Ultrasound in Simple Terms
Think of ultrasound like a bat's echolocation. We send sound waves (too high-pitched for humans to hear) into the body, and they bounce back to create pictures of internal organs. It's completely safe—no radiation like X-rays.

**Technical Definition:** Medical ultrasonography uses high-frequency sound waves (2-15 MHz) to create real-time images of internal body structures through acoustic reflection and transmission properties of tissues.

### Echocardiography (Echo) in Simple Terms  
Echo is ultrasound specifically focused on the heart. We can see the heart beating in real-time, watch blood flow, and measure how well the heart pumps. It's like watching a live movie of the heart.

**Technical Definition:** Echocardiography is a specialized form of cardiac ultrasonography that evaluates heart structure, function, and hemodynamics using multiple acoustic windows and imaging modalities.

### Electrocardiography (EKG/ECG) in Simple Terms
EKG records the heart's electrical signals. Every heartbeat starts with an electrical impulse—we capture these signals through electrodes on the skin to see heart rhythm and detect abnormalities.

**Technical Definition:** Electrocardiography measures the electrical activity of the heart through surface electrodes, recording depolarization and repolarization patterns of cardiac tissue.

## 1.2 Your Role vs. Clinician Interpretation

### What YOU Do as a Technician:
- **Acquire images/data** safely and accurately
- **Optimize image quality** through proper technique
- **Ensure patient comfort** and dignity
- **Document findings** without interpretation
- **Recognize when to stop** and escalate immediately
- **Maintain equipment** and infection control

### What YOU Do NOT Do:
- **Diagnose** conditions or disease
- **Interpret** clinical significance
- **Provide medical advice** to patients
- **Make treatment recommendations**
- **Discuss findings** beyond basic technical explanations

**Remember:** You are the eyes and hands for the clinician. Your job is to capture the best possible data for their interpretation.

## 1.3 How Each Modality Helps Diagnose or Triage

### Ultrasound Applications:
- **Emergency assessment:** Gallbladder, kidney stones, fluid collections
- **Vascular access guidance:** Central line placement, IV insertion
- **Soft tissue evaluation:** Abscess detection, foreign bodies
- **Basic organ screening:** Liver, kidneys, bladder

### Echocardiography Applications:
- **Heart function:** Ejection fraction, wall motion
- **Emergency cardiac assessment:** Pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade
- **Volume status:** IVC assessment for fluid management
- **Basic valve function:** Regurgitation, stenosis

### EKG Applications:
- **Rhythm analysis:** Atrial fibrillation, heart blocks
- **Acute coronary syndromes:** ST elevation MI, unstable angina
- **Emergency conditions:** Life-threatening arrhythmias
- **Baseline monitoring:** Pre/post procedure assessment

## 1.4 Basic Physics at Technician Level

### Ultrasound Physics Fundamentals

**Frequency:** Higher frequency = better resolution but less penetration
- **High frequency (7-15 MHz):** Superficial structures (thyroid, soft tissue)
- **Low frequency (2-5 MHz):** Deep structures (abdomen, heart)

**Penetration:** How deep sound waves travel
- **Tissue-dependent:** Bone stops sound, fluid transmits well
- **Frequency trade-off:** Can't have both high resolution AND deep penetration

**Doppler Basics:** Detects moving objects (blood flow)
- **Red = Toward** the probe (artery)
- **Blue = Away** from the probe (vein)
- **Mnemonic:** BART (Blue Away, Red Toward)

### EKG Conduction Basics

**Normal Electrical Pathway:**
1. **SA Node** (natural pacemaker) → starts the heartbeat
2. **AV Node** → delays signal, allows atria to empty
3. **Bundle of His** → conducts to ventricles
4. **Purkinje fibers** → spreads throughout ventricles

**What We Measure:**
- **P wave:** Atrial depolarization (contraction)
- **QRS complex:** Ventricular depolarization (contraction)
- **T wave:** Ventricular repolarization (relaxation)

## 1.5 Safety Protocols

### ALARA Principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)
- **Minimize ultrasound exposure time** to achieve diagnostic images
- **Use lowest power settings** that provide adequate image quality
- **Avoid prolonged scanning** over fetal tissue or eyes
- **No confirmed biological effects** at diagnostic power levels, but prudence is key

### Infection Control & Probe Disinfection

**Between Every Patient:**
1. **Remove gel** with appropriate wipes
2. **Disinfect probe** with facility-approved solution
3. **Allow contact time** as per manufacturer guidelines
4. **Air dry completely** before next use

**High-Level Disinfection Required:**
- **Transvaginal probes**
- **Transesophageal probes** 
- **Any probe** contacting mucous membranes

### EKG Electrical Safety
- **Inspect cables** for damage before each use
- **Ensure proper grounding** of equipment
- **Avoid wet conditions** during electrode placement
- **Never use** damaged or frayed electrodes
- **Check for pacemakers** and adjust settings accordingly

## 1.6 Patient Interaction Fundamentals

### Consent and Identification
**Every Exam Starts With:**
1. **Verify patient identity** with two identifiers (name + DOB)
2. **Confirm exam ordered** matches patient
3. **Explain procedure** in simple terms
4. **Obtain verbal consent** for examination
5. **Answer basic questions** about comfort and positioning

### Dignity and Comfort
- **Provide appropriate draping** at all times
- **Explain each step** before touching patient
- **Minimize exposure** to only necessary areas
- **Offer chaperone** when appropriate
- **Respect cultural/religious concerns**

### Communication Scripts
**Starting an Exam:**
"Hello, I'm [Name], and I'll be performing your ultrasound/EKG today. I need to verify your name and date of birth first."

**Explaining Ultrasound:**
"This is an ultrasound exam where I'll use sound waves to look at [organ/area]. The gel might feel cold, and I'll need to press gently with the probe. It shouldn't hurt."

**Explaining EKG:**
"I'll place sticky electrodes on your chest, arms, and legs to record your heart's electrical activity. It's painless and takes about 5 minutes."

### Contraindications & "STOP/ESCALATE NOW" Situations

**Immediate Escalation Required:**
- **Patient in distress** or reports severe pain
- **Allergic reaction** to gel or electrodes
- **Hemodynamic instability** during exam
- **Equipment malfunction** affecting patient safety
- **Patient refuses** or cannot tolerate positioning

**Ultrasound-Specific Red Flags:**
- **Abdominal pain** with peritoneal signs during scanning
- **Suspected AAA** with expansion or symptoms
- **Cardiac arrest** rhythm during echo

**EKG-Specific Red Flags:**
- **Ventricular fibrillation** or ventricular tachycardia
- **Complete heart block** with symptoms
- **STEMI** patterns requiring immediate intervention

# 2. Essential Anatomy With Real Images

## 2.1 Ultrasound-Relevant Anatomy by Region

### Abdominal Anatomy

![Gray's Anatomy Abdominal Organs](https://www.bartleby.com/107/illus492.jpg)
*Source: Henry Gray (1918). Anatomy of the Human Body, Fig. 492*  
*License: Public Domain*  
*Original source: https://www.bartleby.com/107/*

**What You Must Know:**
- **Liver location:** Right upper quadrant, largest organ
- **Gallbladder position:** Underneath liver, pear-shaped
- **Right kidney:** Retroperitoneal, posterior to liver
- **Inferior vena cava:** Returns blood to heart, compressible
- **Aorta:** Main artery, pulsatile, non-compressible

**What You Do NOT Need to Memorize Yet:**
- Detailed hepatic segmentation
- Complex vascular anatomy
- Pancreatic duct systems

**Common Beginner Misunderstandings:**
- **Gallbladder vs. kidney:** GB is more anterior and mobile
- **IVC vs. aorta:** IVC collapses with inspiration, aorta doesn't
- **Liver edge:** Normal liver shouldn't extend below costal margin

### Cardiac Anatomy for Echo

![Heart Chambers from Gray's Anatomy](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Gray490.png)
*Source: Henry Gray (1918). Anatomy of the Human Body*  
*License: Public Domain*  
*Original source: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Gray490.png*

**Four-Chamber Heart Layout:**
- **Right atrium:** Receives blood from body (IVC/SVC)
- **Right ventricle:** Pumps blood to lungs via pulmonary artery
- **Left atrium:** Receives blood from lungs via pulmonary veins
- **Left ventricle:** Pumps blood to body via aorta

**What You Must Know:**
- **Chamber positions:** RA/RV on patient's right, LA/LV on left
- **Valve locations:** Tricuspid (RA-RV), Mitral (LA-LV), Aortic, Pulmonary
- **Wall thickness:** LV thicker than RV (higher pressure system)
- **Septum:** Wall separating right and left sides

**What You Do NOT Need to Memorize Yet:**
- Papillary muscle anatomy
- Coronary artery distributions
- Complex valve mechanics

## 2.2 Electrical Conduction System for EKG

![Heart Conduction System](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/ConductionsystemoftheheartwithouttheHeart.png/512px-ConductionsystemoftheheartwithouttheHeart.png)
*Source: Wikimedia Commons*  
*Author: J. Heuser*  
*License: CC BY-SA 3.0*  
*Original source: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:ConductionsystemoftheheartwithouttheHeart.png*

**Electrical Pathway Sequence:**
1. **SA Node** (sinoatrial node) - Natural pacemaker
2. **Atrial pathways** - Spreads through atria
3. **AV Node** (atrioventricular node) - Gateway to ventricles
4. **Bundle of His** - Main highway to ventricles
5. **Bundle branches** - Left and right pathways
6. **Purkinje fibers** - Final distribution network

**What You Must Know:**
- **Normal heart rate:** SA node fires 60-100 times/minute
- **AV delay:** Allows atria to empty before ventricles contract
- **Lead placement** captures electrical activity from different angles

**Common Beginner Misunderstandings:**
- **EKG shows electrical activity,** not mechanical pumping
- **Different leads** show the same heartbeat from different viewpoints
- **Artifact** can mimic real rhythms—always check the patient

**Ultrasound:**
- RUQ pain (gallbladder evaluation)
- Renal colic (kidney stone assessment)
- Pelvic pain (ovarian/uterine evaluation)
- Shortness of breath (pleural effusion)

### Two-Point Identification REQUIRED
**Primary identifiers** (choose two):
1. **Full name** (first and last)
2. **Date of birth** 
3. **Medical record number**

**DO NOT USE as identifiers:**
- Room number (patients move)
- Age (can be calculated incorrectly)  
- Diagnosis
- Bed assignment

"Before we begin, I need to verify your identity for safety. Can you please tell me your full name and date of birth?"

**If patient unable to respond:**
- Check ID bracelet with two identifiers
- Verify with family member if present

- Document verification method used

## 4.5 Patient Positioning and Draping

### Universal Positioning Principles
- **Explain positioning** before moving patient
- **Assist safely** with position changes
- **Minimize discomfort** whenever possible
- **Maintain dignity** with appropriate draping
- **Ensure patient stability** in all positions

### Ultrasound Positioning by Exam Type

**Abdominal/Gallbladder:**
- **Position:** Supine initially, left lateral decubitus if needed
- **Preparation:** NPO status verified (fasting for GB)
- **Breathing:** Coach breath-holding during acquisition

**Renal/Bladder:**
- **Position:** Supine and prone for kidneys
- **Preparation:** Full bladder helpful for pelvic exams
- **Comfort:** Pillow under knees for comfort

**Thyroid/Neck:**
- **Position:** Supine with neck extended
- **Support:** Small pillow under shoulders
- **Caution:** Monitor for breathing difficulty

### Echo Positioning
**Standard positions:**
- **Left lateral decubitus:** Primary position for most views
- **Supine:** For subcostal and apical views
- **Right lateral:** Occasionally for difficult windows

### EKG Positioning
- **Supine preferred:** Most accurate baseline
- **Arms at sides:** Reduces muscle artifact  
- **Legs uncrossed:** Prevents electrical interference
- **Comfortable position:** Patient relaxation important

## 4.6 Documentation Basics and Common Charting Traps

### Documentation Requirements
**Every exam must include:**
- Date and time of examination
- Patient identification verification method
- Indication for exam
- Images acquired with proper labeling
- Technical quality assessment
- Patient tolerance of procedure
- Any limitations encountered
- Technician identification

### What to Document vs. What NOT to Document

**DO Document:**
- Technical observations ("poor acoustic window")
- Patient cooperation ("patient unable to hold breath")  
- Image quality ("limited by body habitus")
- Equipment issues ("probe malfunction noted")
- Safety concerns ("patient reported chest pain during exam")

**DO NOT Document:**
- Clinical interpretations ("gallbladder appears inflamed")
- Diagnostic conclusions ("rule out MI")
- Treatment recommendations ("patient needs surgery")
- Medical advice given to patient
- Comparison to prior studies (unless specifically trained)

### Common Documentation Traps

**Trap #1: Over-interpreting findings**
- **Wrong:** "Liver looks cirrhotic"
- **Right:** "Liver shows coarse echotexture"

**Trap #2: Using diagnostic language**
- **Wrong:** "No pneumothorax seen"  
- **Right:** "Pleural interface appears normal"

**Trap #3: Incomplete technical documentation**
- **Wrong:** "Normal exam"
- **Right:** "All standard views obtained with good image quality"

**Trap #4: Failing to document limitations**
- **Wrong:** No mention of poor windows
- **Right:** "Limited by overlying bowel gas in RUQ views"

# 5. Portable Ultrasound Scanning Guide

## 5.1 Scanning Fundamentals

### Probe Orientation Basics

**Standard Orientation Marker:**
- **Indicator** (usually a groove or light) on probe
- **Screen marker** corresponds to probe indicator
- **Standard position:** Indicator toward patient's head/right side

**Three Basic Planes:**
1. **Sagittal:** Divides body into left and right halves
2. **Transverse:** Divides body into upper and lower halves  
3. **Coronal:** Divides body into front and back halves

### Sweep Patterns for Complete Coverage

**Systematic Scanning Pattern:**
1. **Long axis view** first (sagittal orientation)
2. **Short axis view** second (transverse orientation)  
3. **Fan through** entire structure slowly
4. **Rotate probe** 90 degrees between orientations
5. **Document** representative images from each view

**Golden Rule:** Never diagnose from one image. Always scan in multiple planes.

### Gel Application Technique
- **Liberal amount:** More gel = better contact
- **Warm gel** when possible for patient comfort
- **Spread evenly** before placing probe
- **Reapply** as needed during exam
- **Remove completely** after exam

### Basic Probe Manipulation

**Pressure Control:**
- **Light contact:** Start gently, increase as needed
- **Steady pressure:** Avoid bouncing or jerky movements
- **Patient feedback:** Ask about comfort level

**Angling and Tilting:**
- **Heel-toe motion:** Tilt probe to optimize beam angle
- **Side-to-side:** Rock probe to see around structures
- **Rotation:** Turn probe to change image plane

## 5.2 Artifact Recognition

Understanding artifacts prevents misdiagnosis and improves image quality.

### Acoustic Shadowing
**Appearance:** Dark area behind bright structure
**Cause:** Sound absorbed or reflected completely
**Common locations:** Behind gallstones, bone, gas
**Clinical significance:** Can hide pathology behind stones

### Posterior Enhancement  
**Appearance:** Brighter area behind fluid-filled structure
**Cause:** Less sound attenuation through fluid
**Common locations:** Behind bladder, gallbladder, cysts
**Clinical significance:** Helps confirm fluid-filled structures

### Reverberation Artifact
**Appearance:** Multiple parallel echoes at regular intervals  
**Cause:** Sound bouncing between two strong reflectors
**Common locations:** Lung surface, metallic objects
**Clinical significance:** Can mimic pathology

### Mirror Image Artifact
**Appearance:** Structure appears duplicated
**Cause:** Sound reflects off curved surface (diaphragm)
**Common locations:** Above diaphragm
**Clinical significance:** Liver can appear in chest

### Side Lobe Artifact
**Appearance:** Structures appear where they shouldn't be
**Cause:** Off-axis sound beams
**Common locations:** Gallbladder with adjacent structures
**Clinical significance:** Can mimic gallbladder pathology

## 5.3 Image Optimization Step-by-Step

When images look poor, follow this systematic approach:

### Step 1: Patient Positioning
- Try different patient positions
- Use breath-holding when appropriate
- Optimize acoustic windows

### Step 2: Probe Selection and Pressure
- Confirm correct probe for depth needed
- Adjust contact pressure
- Ensure adequate gel

### Step 3: Basic Controls Adjustment  

**Depth Adjustment:**
- Set depth to include area of interest
- Don't scan deeper than necessary
- Adjust to optimize image size

**Gain Optimization:**
- Overall gain affects entire image brightness
- Start with manufacturer presets
- Adjust until optimal contrast

**TGC (Time Gain Compensation):**
- Adjust individual depth zone brightness
- Compensate for normal sound attenuation
- Create uniform brightness throughout image

### Step 4: Advanced Optimization

**Focus Position:**
- Set focus at level of main interest
- Can use multiple focus zones
- Impacts resolution at focus level

**Frequency Selection:**
- Higher frequency for better resolution (if depth allows)
- Lower frequency for better penetration
- Modern probes auto-adjust

### Step 5: When to Move On
**Image is adequate when:**
- Structure of interest clearly visible
- Adequate brightness throughout
- Minimal artifacts affecting diagnosis
- Patient has tolerated maximum optimization time

**Document limitations when:**
- Patient body habitus limits visualization
- Bowel gas obscures structures
- Patient cannot cooperate with positioning
- Equipment limitations affect image quality

# 6. Focused Echocardiography Guide

## 6.1 Echo Goals vs. Full Cardiology Echo

### Purpose of Focused Echo (POCUS Cardiac)
**Primary goals:**
- **Binary questions:** Is there major dysfunction? Yes/No
- **Emergency assessment:** Pericardial effusion, severe dysfunction
- **Volume status:** Is the patient fluid overloaded or depleted?
- **Basic function:** Is the heart squeezing well globally?

### What Focused Echo IS NOT
- **Complete diagnostic study** with detailed measurements
- **Valve assessment** for surgical planning  
- **Comprehensive evaluation** of all cardiac structures
- **Substitute for formal echocardiogram** when indicated

### Scope Limitations
**Stay within your training level:**
- Recognize gross abnormalities
- Identify when formal echo is needed
- Notify clinician of concerning findings
- Document limitations clearly

## 6.2 Patient Positioning and Probe Setup

### Optimal Patient Positioning
**Left lateral decubitus position:**
- Patient lies on left side
- Brings heart closer to chest wall
- Improves acoustic windows
- Most comfortable for extended scanning

**Alternative positions when needed:**
- **Supine:** For subcostal views, larger patients
- **Sitting up:** For difficult windows, respiratory distress
- **Right lateral:** Rarely needed for specific windows

### ECG Gating for Clips
**Why ECG gating matters:**
- Allows measurement at specific cardiac cycle phases
- Ensures consistent timing for clips
- Helps identify arrhythmias during scanning

**Setup:**
1. Place 3 ECG electrodes on patient
2. Connect to ultrasound machine
3. Verify good signal quality before scanning
4. Adjust gain for clear QRS detection

## 6.3 Standard Echo Views - Step by Step

### Parasternal Long Axis (PLAX) View

![Echo PLAX View Probe Position](https://www.echocardiographer.org/PlaxProbe.gif)
*Illustration showing proper probe placement for PLAX view*  
*Note: Real probe placement photo needed - searching for appropriate Creative Commons licensed image*

**Probe Placement:**
- **Location:** Left parasternal border, 3rd-4th intercostal space
- **Indicator:** Points toward patient's right shoulder (1-2 o'clock)
- **Angle:** Aim beam toward right scapula
- **Contact:** Gentle pressure, avoid sliding on ribs

**Sweep/Fanning Steps:**
1. **Initial image:** Center heart in display
2. **Optimize depth:** Include entire heart, some lung
3. **Adjust gain:** See endocardial borders clearly
4. **Fine-tune angle:** Maximize chamber length
5. **Acquire clips:** 3-5 cardiac cycles with good ECG

**Structures That Must Be Visible:**
- **Right ventricle:** Anterior (closest to probe)
- **Left ventricle:** Posterior chamber
- **Left atrium:** Posterior to LV
- **Aortic root:** Between RV and LA
- **Mitral valve:** Between LA and LV
- **Pericardium:** Bright line around heart

**"Looks Right" vs "Looks Wrong":**

**Good PLAX qualities:**
- Heart centered and maximized
- All chambers clearly visible  
- Endocardial borders well-defined
- Minimal artifacts

**Poor PLAX qualities:**
- Heart cut off or off-center
- Chambers obliqued or foreshortened
- Heavy artifacts obscuring structures
- Probe position clearly incorrect

**Common Errors and Fixes:**
- **Too lateral:** Move probe medially toward sternum
- **Wrong intercostal space:** Move up or down one space  
- **Wrong angle:** Adjust probe direction toward scapula
- **Poor contact:** Add gel, optimize pressure

### Parasternal Short Axis (PSAX) Views

**Base Level (Aortic Valve Level):**
**Probe placement:** Rotate probe 90° clockwise from PLAX
**Key structures:** Aortic valve (center), pulmonary valve, tricuspid valve, atria

**Mid Level (Mitral Valve Level):**
**Probe placement:** Same as base, angle slightly toward apex
**Key structures:** Mitral valve opening, LV walls in cross-section

**Papillary Level:**
**Probe placement:** Angle further toward apex from mid level
**Key structures:** Papillary muscles, circular LV cross-section

**Acquisition Steps for All PSAX Levels:**
1. **Start from good PLAX**
2. **Rotate probe 90° clockwise**
3. **Adjust depth and gain**
4. **Fan from base to apex** slowly
5. **Acquire clips at each level**

### Apical 4-Chamber View

**Probe Placement:**
- **Location:** Cardiac apex (point of maximal impulse)
- **Indicator:** Points to patient's left side (9 o'clock)
- **Angle:** Aim toward right shoulder
- **Contact:** May need firm pressure to get between ribs

**Sweep/Fanning Steps:**
1. **Find cardiac apex** by palpation
2. **Place probe at apex** with indicator left
3. **Angle beam** toward base of heart
4. **Optimize to see all 4 chambers**
5. **Ensure chambers not foreshortened**

**Structures That Must Be Visible:**
- **Right atrium:** Patient's left side of screen
- **Right ventricle:** Below RA
- **Left atrium:** Patient's right side of screen  
- **Left ventricle:** Below LA
- **Tricuspid valve:** Between RA and RV
- **Mitral valve:** Between LA and LV

### Apical 2-Chamber View

**Probe placement:** Rotate 90° counterclockwise from apical 4-chamber
**Key structures:** LA and LV only, anterior and inferior LV walls

### Apical 3-Chamber View (Long Axis)

**Probe placement:** Rotate 60° counterclockwise from apical 2-chamber  
**Key structures:** LA, LV, aortic root and valve, similar to PLAX but from apex

### Subcostal 4-Chamber View

**Probe Placement:**
- **Location:** Subxiphoid, below sternum
- **Indicator:** Points to patient's left side
- **Angle:** Aim beam toward left shoulder
- **Pressure:** Often need significant pressure to penetrate

**When to use subcostal:**
- Poor parasternal windows
- Mechanical ventilation  
- Post-operative patients with chest tubes
- Severe COPD with hyperinflated lungs

### IVC (Inferior Vena Cava) View

**Probe Placement:**
- **Location:** Subxiphoid, similar to subcostal 4-chamber
- **Indicator:** Points to patient's head (12 o'clock)
- **Angle:** Aim toward spine, parallel to patient
- **Depth:** Usually 15-20 cm

**Assessment:**
- **Measure IVC diameter** 2-3 cm from RA junction
- **Assess collapsibility** with inspiration
- **Normal:** >50% collapse with sniff
- **Elevated pressures:** <50% collapse

## 6.4 Required Stills and Clips for Documentation

### Standard Acquisition Protocol

**PLAX view:**
- **Still images:** End-diastole and end-systole
- **Clips:** 3-5 cardiac cycles with clear ECG
- **Measurements:** LVEDD, LVESD if trained

**PSAX views:**
- **Base level:** Aortic valve clips
- **Mid level:** Mitral valve motion clips  
- **Papillary level:** LV wall motion clips

**Apical views:**
- **4-chamber:** Clips showing all 4 chambers
- **2-chamber:** Clips of LA and LV
- **3-chamber:** Clips including aortic valve

**Subcostal:**
- **4-chamber:** Alternative window clips
- **IVC:** Clips showing respiratory variation

### Quality Standards for Clips
- **Duration:** 3-5 cardiac cycles minimum
- **ECG quality:** Clear QRS complexes visible
- **Image stability:** Minimal probe movement
- **Depth optimization:** Heart fills most of screen
- **Frame rate:** >30 fps for wall motion analysis

# 7. EKG Acquisition Guide

## 7.1 What EKG Measures (Simple Then Detailed)

### Simple Explanation
An EKG measures the heart's electrical signals that tell it when to beat. Every heartbeat starts with an electrical impulse, and we can record these signals through sticky electrodes on the skin.

### Detailed Explanation  
**Electrical Activity Measured:**
- **Depolarization:** Electrical activation causing muscle contraction
- **Repolarization:** Electrical recovery preparing for next beat
- **Conduction:** How electrical signals travel through heart tissue
- **Rhythm:** Pattern and timing of electrical activation

**What Each Wave Represents:**
- **P wave:** Atrial depolarization (atria contract)
- **QRS complex:** Ventricular depolarization (ventricles contract)  
- **T wave:** Ventricular repolarization (ventricles relax)
- **U wave:** Late repolarization (not always present)

## 7.2 Patient Preparation

### Skin Cleaning and Preparation
**Steps for optimal contact:**
1. **Expose necessary areas** with appropriate draping
2. **Clean skin** with alcohol prep pads
3. **Allow to dry** completely before electrode placement
4. **Abrade lightly** with gauze if very oily skin
5. **Shave hair** if thick enough to prevent contact

### Hair Removal Technique
**When shaving is needed:**
- **Use disposable razors** for each patient
- **Shave minimal area** needed for electrode contact
- **Clean area** after shaving to remove loose hair
- **Inform patient** before hair removal
- **Document** if significant hair removal required

### Electrode Gel and Allergy Considerations
**Electrode preparation:**
- **Check expiration dates** before use
- **Ensure gel is moist** - discard dry electrodes
- **Note latex allergies** - use latex-free electrodes
- **Observe for skin reactions** during placement
- **Document allergies** observed during procedure

## 7.4 Precordial Leads V1-V6 Placement

### Anatomical Landmarks for Chest Leads

**Finding the Intercostal Spaces:**
1. **Locate sternal notch** (suprasternal notch)
2. **Find sternal angle** (Angle of Louis) - bony ridge
3. **Sternal angle = 2nd intercostal space**
4. **Count down** to find other spaces
5. **Follow ribs laterally** for V4-V6 placement

### Precise V-Lead Positions

**V1:** 4th intercostal space, right sternal border
**V2:** 4th intercostal space, left sternal border
**V3:** Midway between V2 and V4
**V4:** 5th intercostal space, left midclavicular line
**V5:** Same horizontal level as V4, left anterior axillary line  
**V6:** Same horizontal level as V4, left midaxillary line

### Critical Placement Points

**V1 and V2:**
- **Must be in 4th ICS** - not higher or lower
- **Right on sternal border** - not on sternum itself
- **Symmetric placement** - same level on both sides

**V4 Location is Key:**
- **5th ICS** confirmed by counting from sternal angle
- **Midclavicular line** - halfway down clavicle
- **If breast tissue:** place electrode under breast, same anatomical location

**V3, V5, V6:**
- **Same horizontal level** as V4
- **V3:** Exactly midway between V2 and V4  
- **V5:** Anterior axillary line (front armpit fold)
- **V6:** Mid-axillary line (middle of armpit)

### Placement Verification Checklist
□ **V1-V2:** 4th ICS, sternal borders
□ **V4:** 5th ICS, midclavicular line  
□ **V3:** Midway between V2 and V4
□ **V5-V6:** Same level as V4, proper axillary lines
□ **All electrodes:** Good skin contact, no air bubbles
□ **Symmetric placement:** Left and right sides mirror each other

## 7.6 Artifact Recognition & Correction

### Muscle Tremor Artifact

**Appearance:** Irregular, fuzzy baseline with varying amplitude
**Causes:** Patient anxiety, cold, Parkinson's disease, caffeine
**Corrections:**
1. **Warm room** and patient
2. **Reassure anxious** patients
3. **Support arms** on pillows
4. **Reposition electrodes** if local tremor
5. **Document** if artifact persists despite interventions

### Wandering Baseline

**Appearance:** Baseline drifts up and down slowly
**Causes:** Poor electrode contact, patient movement, breathing
**Corrections:**
1. **Check electrode contact** - replace if dry
2. **Clean skin** more thoroughly
3. **Ensure patient stillness**
4. **Check lead connections**
5. **Replace electrodes** if problem persists

### 60-Cycle Interference

**Appearance:** Regular, fine oscillations at 60 Hz
**Causes:** Electrical interference from power lines, equipment
**Corrections:**
1. **Unplug nearby** electrical equipment
2. **Move away** from transformers, monitors
3. **Check grounding** of EKG machine
4. **Ensure proper** electrode contact
5. **Use filters** if available on machine

### Loose Lead Detection

**Appearance:** Straight line or wild oscillations in specific leads
**Causes:** Disconnected wire, poor electrode contact
**Corrections:**
1. **Check all connections** systematically
2. **Replace loose electrodes**
3. **Clean electrode sites** and reapply
4. **Inspect lead wires** for damage
5. **Test machine** with different lead set

### Reversed Lead Recognition

**Appearance:** Unexpected wave morphologies, unusual axis
**Common reversals:**
- **RA-LA reversal:** Lead I inverted, aVR looks like normal aVL
- **Limb-precordial mix:** QRS in limb leads look like chest leads
**Prevention:**
- **Double-check color coding** before connecting
- **Follow systematic** placement routine
- **Verify lead positions** before recording

## 7.7 Rhythm Strip Basics

### Standard Rhythm Strip Protocol
**Lead II rhythm strip:**
- **Duration:** 6-10 seconds minimum
- **Quality:** Clear baseline, good QRS definition
- **Purpose:** Heart rhythm and rate analysis
- **Timing:** Usually recorded automatically at end

### Basic Rhythm Assessment (Recognition Only)
**Normal Sinus Rhythm:**
- **Rate:** 60-100 BPM
- **Rhythm:** Regular R-R intervals
- **P waves:** Present before each QRS
- **QRS:** Narrow (<0.12 seconds)

**Immediately Report These Rhythms:**
- **Ventricular fibrillation:** Chaotic, no organized beats
- **Ventricular tachycardia:** Wide QRS, rate >150
- **Asystole:** Flat line, no electrical activity
- **Complete heart block:** P waves and QRS independent

**Remember:** Your job is recognition and immediate notification, not interpretation.

# 8. Real-Life Situations & Troubleshooting

## 8.1 Poor/Unclean Ultrasound Images: Systematic Optimization

When ultrasound images look suboptimal, follow this systematic ladder approach. Each step builds on the previous one.

### Step 1: Reposition Patient
**Try these positions systematically:**
- **Different breathing phases:** Full inspiration, expiration, breath hold
- **Body position changes:** Supine to left/right decubitus
- **Trendelenburg:** Head down to redistribute organs
- **Reverse Trendelenburg:** Head up for different organ positions
- **Standing position:** For gallbladder mobility (if patient able)

**What you're looking for:** Improved acoustic windows, decreased overlying structures

**How to know if it worked:** Clearer definition of organ borders, less shadowing

### Step 2: Change Probe / Preset
**Probe selection strategy:**
- **Higher frequency probe:** If structure superficial (<6 cm)
- **Lower frequency probe:** If deeper penetration needed
- **Different probe type:** Curved vs linear based on field of view needed

**Preset optimization:**
- **Organ-specific presets:** Use appropriate preset for structure
- **Depth-specific presets:** Optimize for scanning depth
- **Custom settings:** Adjust from preset baseline if needed

**What you're looking for:** Better penetration or resolution match for structure

### Step 3: Adjust Depth
**Depth optimization rules:**
- **Include area of interest** plus 2-3 cm beyond
- **Don't scan deeper** than necessary (wastes time and resolution)
- **Shallow depth:** Better resolution for superficial structures
- **Appropriate depth:** Maximizes image size on screen

**How to know optimal depth:** Structure of interest occupies 60-80% of screen depth

### Step 4: Adjust Gain/TGC
**Overall gain adjustment:**
- **Too bright:** Decrease gain, may lose detail in shadows
- **Too dark:** Increase gain, but don't mask pathology  
- **Optimal:** Can see subtle tissue differences

**TGC (Time Gain Compensation):**
- **Adjust individual depth zones** to compensate for attenuation
- **Goal:** Uniform echogenicity throughout image depth
- **Common pattern:** Increase gain with depth to compensate for attenuation

**What you're looking for:** Uniform tissue brightness, good contrast resolution

### Step 5: Adjust Focus
**Focus position rules:**
- **Set focus** at level of main area of interest
- **Multiple focus zones:** Can improve resolution throughout image
- **Focus too shallow:** Poor resolution at structure level  
- **Focus too deep:** Poor resolution at structure level

**How to know optimal focus:** Best lateral resolution at level of interest

### Step 6: Change Window
**Alternative acoustic windows:**
- **Different intercostal spaces:** For cardiac imaging
- **Different patient positions:** To move overlying structures
- **Different probe angles:** To see around obstacles
- **Subcostal approach:** When intercostal windows poor

**When to try different windows:** When overlying structures (ribs, gas, tissue) block optimal view

### Step 7: Use Breath Holds
**Breath hold technique:**
- **Explain to patient:** "Take a breath and hold it"
- **Practice first:** Ensure patient understands
- **Optimal phase:** Usually inspiration for abdominal organs
- **Short duration:** 5-10 seconds maximum
- **Multiple attempts:** Try several times if needed

**What breath holds accomplish:** Moves diaphragm, repositions organs, stops motion artifacts

### Step 8: Document Limitation
**When optimization fails, document:**
- **"Limited by body habitus"** - obesity preventing penetration
- **"Limited by overlying bowel gas"** - gas blocking view
- **"Limited by patient cooperation"** - unable to position or breath hold
- **"Limited by rib shadowing"** - bony interference
- **"Limited by prior surgical changes"** - adhesions, clips affecting view

**Documentation prevents:** Inappropriate repeat orders, questions about missed pathology

## 8.2 Echo Poor Windows Scenarios

### Obese Patient Strategies

**Problem:** Sound attenuation through thick tissue layers
**Solutions in order of preference:**
1. **Harmonic imaging:** Reduces artifact, improves penetration
2. **Lower frequency:** 2-3 MHz for better penetration
3. **Increase power:** If available on machine
4. **Subcostal window:** Often better in obese patients
5. **Patient positioning:** Left lateral with roll under left side
6. **Extended scan time:** Allow for optimization attempts

**Documentation:** "Technically limited study due to body habitus. [Specific views] not adequately visualized."

### COPD/Hyperinflated Lungs

**Problem:** Air-filled lungs block sound transmission
**Alternative approaches:**
1. **Subcostal window:** Primary alternative for most views
2. **Very low probe position:** Below hyperinflated lungs
3. **Right parasternal:** Sometimes better than left
4. **Patient sitting up:** May compress lungs slightly
5. **Expiration imaging:** Less lung hyperinflation

**Specific adaptations:**
- **Apical views:** May need much lower and lateral probe position
- **Parasternal views:** Often completely blocked
- **Subcostal:** Usually becomes primary imaging window

### Tachycardia (High Heart Rate)

**Problem:** Rapid heart rate makes assessment difficult
**Adaptations:**
1. **Increase frame rate:** Capture rapid motion better
2. **Shorter clips:** 2-3 seconds may be adequate
3. **Focus on global function:** Don't attempt detailed measurements
4. **Multiple short acquisitions:** Instead of long clips
5. **Consider rate control:** If clinically appropriate and ordered

**What becomes difficult:** Diastolic assessment, precise measurements, valve evaluation

### Cannot Lie Left Lateral

**Problem:** Patient unable to achieve optimal positioning
**Alternative strategies:**
1. **Supine imaging:** Start with what patient can tolerate
2. **Right lateral:** Occasionally helpful for specific views
3. **Semi-upright:** 30-45 degree elevation
4. **Subcostal emphasis:** Usually tolerated in any position
5. **Shortened exam:** Focus on most critical views only

**Position modifications:**
- **Support with pillows:** Make position as comfortable as possible
- **Frequent position changes:** Brief attempts at optimal positioning
- **Work with what you have:** Don't force uncomfortable positions

### Post-Op Bandages/Dressings

**Problem:** Surgical dressings blocking acoustic windows
**Approach:**
1. **Work around dressings:** Don't remove without permission
2. **Alternative windows:** Use unaffected areas
3. **Subcostal approach:** Usually not affected by chest dressings
4. **Notify nursing:** If dressing modification needed for critical exam
5. **Document limitations:** Clear documentation of restricted access

**Never remove:** Surgical dressings without specific orders and nursing coordination

## 8.3 EKG Messy Data Scenarios

### Sweaty Skin/Poor Adhesion

**Problem:** Electrodes won't stick or maintain contact
**Step-by-step solution:**
1. **Dry skin thoroughly:** Use towels first
2. **Alcohol prep:** Clean and degrease skin
3. **Allow complete drying:** Wait for alcohol evaporation
4. **Light abrasion:** Gentle scrubbing with gauze
5. **Fresh electrodes:** Use new ones with good gel
6. **Firm pressure:** Apply electrodes with 10-second pressure
7. **Tape reinforcement:** If available and policy allows

**Environmental control:** Cool room temperature to reduce ongoing sweating

### Tremor or Parkinsonism

**Problem:** Muscle artifact from involuntary movements
**Systematic approach:**
1. **Patient comfort:** Ensure warm, relaxed environment
2. **Arm support:** Pillows under both arms
3. **Medication timing:** Ask if patient due for anti-tremor medications
4. **Alternative lead placement:** Closer to torso may have less artifact
5. **Multiple attempts:** Try several short recordings
6. **Filters:** Use muscle artifact filters if available
7. **Document:** Note tremor limiting study quality

**What usually doesn't work:** Asking patient to "hold still" - tremor is involuntary

### Cold Shivering Patient

**Problem:** Muscle artifact from shivering
**Solutions:**
1. **Warm room:** Increase temperature if possible
2. **Blankets:** Warm patient before electrode placement
3. **Minimal exposure:** Only expose areas needed for electrodes
4. **Quick procedure:** Work efficiently to minimize exposure time
5. **Warm electrodes:** If warmer available
6. **Patient explanation:** Let them know procedure will be brief

**Prevention:** Warm room and patient before starting procedure

### Electrical Interference

**Problem:** 60-cycle interference or other electrical noise
**Troubleshooting steps:**
1. **Identify source:** Look for nearby electrical equipment
2. **Turn off/unplug:** Non-essential electrical devices
3. **Move away from:** Power transformers, monitors, pumps
4. **Check grounding:** Ensure EKG machine properly grounded
5. **Electrode replacement:** Poor contact can worsen interference
6. **Different location:** Try different room if interference severe
7. **Notify biomedical:** If problem persists with equipment

**Common sources:** IV pumps, monitors, cell phone chargers, fluorescent lights

### Patient Won't Hold Still

**Problem:** Movement artifact from anxious or confused patient
**De-escalation approach:**
1. **Explain procedure:** Simple, calm explanation
2. **Reassurance:** "This doesn't hurt, just measures heartbeat"
3. **Family present:** If appropriate and helpful
4. **Distraction:** Conversation during procedure
5. **Quick execution:** Work efficiently
6. **Restraint only if ordered:** Never restrain without physician order
7. **Sedation consideration:** If ordered and appropriate

**When to stop:** If patient becomes combative or extremely distressed

## 8.5 Communication Scripts

### How to Politely Request Repositioning

**Initial request:**
"To get the best images of your [organ], I need you to turn slightly onto your left side. Is that comfortable for you?"

**If patient hesitates:**
"This position helps me see your heart/gallbladder/kidney more clearly. We can try it for just a minute, and if it's uncomfortable, we can adjust."

**For difficult positions:**
"This might feel a little awkward, but it really helps the sound waves get a clear picture. Can we try this for about 30 seconds?"

**When patient cannot comply:**
"That's okay, we'll work with the position that's most comfortable for you. Let me try a different approach."

### How to Coach Breath Holds

**Preparation:**
"In a moment, I'm going to ask you to take a breath and hold it for just a few seconds while I get these pictures. Let's practice first."

**Practice round:**
"Okay, take a normal breath in... and hold it... perfect. Now breathe normally. That's exactly what we'll do."

**During actual acquisition:**
"Take a breath in... hold it... stay very still... perfect... you can breathe now. Great job!"

**For multiple attempts:**
"That was good, but I need one more. Take your time, catch your breath, and we'll try once more."

**If patient struggles:**
"That's okay, just breathe normally. I can work with that."

### How to De-escalate Anxious Patients

**Acknowledge feelings:**
"I can see you're feeling anxious about this. That's completely normal and understandable."

**Explain simply:**
"This test is very safe and doesn't use any radiation. It's just sound waves, like sonar on a submarine."

**Give control:**
"If you need me to stop at any time, just let me know. You're in control here."

**Use reassuring tone:**
"You're doing great. This is exactly how the test should go. Everything looks normal so far."

**Timeline information:**
"We're about halfway done. Just a couple more minutes and we'll be finished."

**For severe anxiety:**
"Would you like me to ask the nurse to stay with you? Or would you prefer we reschedule when you're feeling more comfortable?"

**Distraction technique:**
"Tell me about... [family, hobbies, etc.] while I finish these images."

## 9.5 "Spot-the-Problem" Drills

### Drill 1: Ultrasound Image Quality
**Situation:** You're scanning a gallbladder and the image shows heavy acoustic shadowing that obscures the posterior wall of the gallbladder.

**What is wrong?**  
Possible gallstone or sludge causing acoustic shadowing, OR overlying bowel gas, OR excessive gain settings creating apparent shadowing.

**What 3 steps fix it?**  
1. Change patient position (left lateral decubitus) to move gallbladder and any stones
2. Try breath-holding to move overlying structures  
3. Adjust gain settings to optimize image contrast

**When do you escalate?**  
If you cannot adequately visualize gallbladder walls after optimization attempts, especially if patient has clinical signs of cholecystitis.

### Drill 2: EKG Artifact Problem
**Situation:** Your EKG tracing shows a fuzzy, irregular baseline that makes it difficult to interpret the rhythm.

**What is wrong?**  
Muscle tremor artifact, likely from patient being cold, anxious, or having underlying tremor disorder.

**What 3 steps fix it?**  
1. Warm the patient and room temperature
2. Support patient's arms with pillows to reduce muscle tension
3. Reassure anxious patient and ensure they're comfortable

**When do you escalate?**  
If artifact persists despite interventions and prevents diagnostic EKG, or if patient shows signs of severe underlying tremor requiring medical evaluation.

### Drill 3: Echo Window Problem  
**Situation:** You cannot obtain adequate parasternal long axis views on an obese patient with COPD.

**What is wrong?**  
Poor acoustic windows due to combination of increased soft tissue (obesity) and hyperinflated lungs (COPD) blocking sound transmission.

**What 3 steps fix it?**  
1. Try subcostal approach as primary alternative window
2. Use lower frequency for better penetration  
3. Position patient in left lateral with roll under side to bring heart closer

**When do you escalate?**  
If no adequate windows can be obtained for basic cardiac assessment, notify clinician that echo is technically limited and may need alternative imaging.

### Drill 4: Patient Safety Issue
**Situation:** During abdominal ultrasound, patient suddenly reports severe right upper quadrant pain when you press with the probe.

**What is wrong?**  
Possible acute cholecystitis, gallbladder inflammation, or other acute abdominal pathology. Pain with probe pressure may indicate peritoneal irritation.

**What 3 steps fix it?**  
1. STOP scanning immediately and remove probe pressure
2. Notify nurse/physician immediately of findings  
3. Monitor patient and be prepared to assist with emergency care

**When do you escalate?**  
Immediately - this could represent acute cholecystitis or other surgical emergency requiring immediate evaluation.

### Drill 5: Equipment Malfunction
**Situation:** During echo exam, the ultrasound machine suddenly displays an error message and shuts down.

**What is wrong?**  
Equipment malfunction that could be software error, hardware failure, or power issue.

**What 3 steps fix it?**  
1. Check power connections and restart machine following manufacturer protocols
2. If restart fails, try different machine if available  
3. Document malfunction and notify biomedical engineering

**When do you escalate?**  
If backup equipment not available and exam is urgent, or if patient condition requires immediate assessment.

### Drill 6: Patient Refuses Procedure
**Situation:** Patient becomes very anxious during EKG and asks you to stop, saying they want to leave.

**What is wrong?**  
Patient autonomy issue - patient has right to refuse medical procedures, but may need education about procedure safety.

**What 3 steps fix it?**  
1. Stop procedure immediately and reassure patient
2. Explain procedure is painless and takes only a few minutes
3. Offer to have nurse or family member present for support

**When do you escalate?**  
If patient continues to refuse after reassurance, notify nurse/physician about patient refusal so alternative plans can be made.

## 11.1 One-Page Cheat Sheets

### Ultrasound Quick Reference

**Probe Selection:**
- **Linear (7-15 MHz):** Superficial (<6cm) - thyroid, soft tissue, vessels
- **Curved (2-5 MHz):** Deep abdomen, pelvis, kidney  
- **Phased (2-5 MHz):** Cardiac imaging between ribs

**Image Optimization Order:**
1. **Depth** - include structure + 2-3cm beyond
2. **Gain** - overall brightness  
3. **TGC** - depth-specific brightness
4. **Focus** - set at level of interest
5. **Frequency** - higher for resolution, lower for penetration

**Common Artifacts:**
- **Shadowing:** Dark area behind bright reflector (stones, gas)
- **Enhancement:** Bright area behind fluid (cysts, bladder)
- **Reverberation:** Multiple echoes (lung surface, metallic objects)

**STOP Signals:**
- Severe patient pain during scanning
- Equipment malfunction affecting safety
- Patient hemodynamic instability
- Allergic reaction to gel

---

### Echo Views Quick Reference

**PLAX (Parasternal Long Axis):**
- **Location:** 3rd-4th ICS, left sternal border
- **Indicator:** Right shoulder (1-2 o'clock)
- **Structures:** RV, LV, LA, aortic root

**PSAX (Parasternal Short Axis):**
- **Rotate 90° from PLAX**
- **Base:** Aortic valve level
- **Mid:** Mitral valve level  
- **Papillary:** LV cross-section

**Apical 4-Chamber:**
- **Location:** Cardiac apex (PMI)
- **Indicator:** Patient's left (9 o'clock)
- **Structures:** All 4 chambers, both AV valves

**Subcostal:**
- **Location:** Subxiphoid  
- **Use when:** Poor parasternal windows
- **Alternative:** For COPD, obesity, mechanical ventilation

**IVC View:**
- **From subcostal position**
- **Measure:** 2-3cm from RA junction
- **Normal collapse:** >50% with inspiration

### EKG Lead Placement Quick Reference

**Limb Leads (USA Colors):**
- **RA:** White - Right wrist/shoulder
- **LA:** Black - Left wrist/shoulder  
- **RL:** Green - Right ankle/lower chest (Ground)
- **LL:** Red - Left ankle/lower chest

**Chest Leads:**
- **V1:** 4th ICS, right sternal border
- **V2:** 4th ICS, left sternal border
- **V3:** Midway between V2 and V4  
- **V4:** 5th ICS, midclavicular line
- **V5:** Same level as V4, anterior axillary line
- **V6:** Same level as V4, mid-axillary line

**Finding 4th ICS:**
1. Find sternal notch (top of breastbone)
2. Find sternal angle (bony ridge = 2nd ICS)  
3. Count down: 3rd ICS, then 4th ICS

**Artifact Quick Fixes:**
- **Fuzzy baseline:** Warm patient, support arms
- **60-cycle interference:** Remove electrical devices
- **Wandering baseline:** Clean skin, replace electrodes
- **Straight line:** Check connections, replace electrodes

## 11.2 Glossary

**ALARA:** As Low As Reasonably Achievable - principle for minimizing ultrasound exposure

**Acoustic Window:** Body surface location providing good ultrasound penetration

**Artifact:** False echo or missing echo not representing actual anatomy

**Attenuation:** Weakening of ultrasound as it travels through tissue

**Cine Loop:** Video clip of ultrasound images showing motion over time

**Curvilinear Probe:** Curved transducer for deep imaging with wide field of view

**Depolarization:** Electrical activation of cardiac muscle causing contraction

**Doppler:** Ultrasound technique for detecting moving structures (blood flow)

**Echogenicity:** Ability of tissue to reflect ultrasound (bright = hyperechoic, dark = hypoechoic)

**Focus:** Depth where ultrasound beam is narrowest and resolution is best

**Gain:** Control adjusting overall brightness of ultrasound image

**IVC:** Inferior Vena Cava - large vein returning blood to right atrium

**Linear Probe:** High-frequency straight transducer for superficial structures

**M-Mode:** Motion mode showing movement of structures over time

**NPO:** Nothing by mouth (nil per os) - fasting status

**PLAX:** Parasternal Long Axis view of the heart

**PMI:** Point of Maximal Impulse - where heartbeat felt strongest on chest

**PSAX:** Parasternal Short Axis view of the heart

**QRS Complex:** EKG waveform representing ventricular depolarization

**Repolarization:** Electrical recovery of cardiac muscle after contraction

**SA Node:** Sinoatrial node - heart's natural pacemaker

**TGC:** Time Gain Compensation - depth-specific brightness control

**Transducer:** Ultrasound probe containing piezoelectric crystals

## 11.3 Troubleshooting Quick Reference

### Ultrasound Image Problems

| Problem | Most Likely Cause | First Step | Next Step |
|---------|------------------|------------|-----------|
| Image too dark | Gain too low | Increase overall gain | Check TGC settings |
| Image too bright | Gain too high | Decrease overall gain | Optimize TGC |
| Poor penetration | Wrong probe/frequency | Use lower frequency | Check patient positioning |
| Heavy artifacts | Overlying gas/bone | Reposition patient | Try different window |
| Cannot see structure | Wrong depth setting | Adjust depth appropriately | Change probe angle |
| Fuzzy image quality | Poor probe contact | Add more gel | Check probe pressure |

### EKG Problems

| Problem | Appearance | Cause | Solution |
|---------|------------|-------|---------|
| Muscle artifact | Fuzzy baseline | Tremor, cold, anxiety | Warm patient, support arms |
| 60-cycle interference | Regular oscillations | Electrical devices | Remove electronics nearby |
| Wandering baseline | Drifting up/down | Poor electrode contact | Clean skin, new electrodes |
| Loose lead | Straight line | Disconnected wire | Check all connections |
| Poor QRS | Small, unclear waves | Wrong lead position | Verify lead placement |

### Patient Management Issues

| Situation | Initial Response | Communication | When to Escalate |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|------------------|
| Patient anxiety | Stop, reassure | "This is safe and painless" | If panic or refusal |
| Pain during exam | Stop immediately | "Are you having pain?" | Any severe pain |
| Cannot tolerate position | Try alternatives | "Let's find comfortable position" | If no position works |
| Equipment malfunction | Stop procedure safely | "I need to check equipment" | If no backup available |
| Allergic reaction | Remove offending agent | Call for help immediately | All allergic reactions |
